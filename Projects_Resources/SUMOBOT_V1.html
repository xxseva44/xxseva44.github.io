About SumoBots:
Sumo bots are small, autonomous robots designed to compete in a robotic version of sumo wrestling. The objective is simple: push the opposing robot out of a defined circular ring, called a "dohyo," while staying within the boundaries. These robots typically use sensors, motors, and a microcontroller to detect their opponent and strategize movements. Sumo bots come in various weight classes, from lightweight to heavyweight, and are governed by strict size and design rules to ensure fair play. Often built with a focus on speed, traction, and pushing power, with features like wedge-shaped bodies to get under opponents or high-torque motors for strength. Some employ advanced tactics, such as using infrared sensors to avoid falling off the ring or to track the opponent's location. Sumo bot competitions combine engineering, programming, and strategic thinking.
My SumoBot.
With an ESP32 at the heart of it and a standard arduino uno R3 and it's ATMEGA328P, this sumo bot is equipped with 3 sonar sensors, 2 “line detection” devices, a rear facing infrared based distance sensor, and a display to display live sensor data and visuals. An arduino R3 as a GPIO expander is used, using serial communication between the two to allow for co-operation.
Platform overview:
This SumoBot revolves around the ESP32, a powerful, smaller soc and as a result; this will allow for greater space allocation to other components as this is a smaller microcontroller. The ESP32 also contains greater capabilities in comparison to the ATmega and Atmel based arduinos while being smaller allowing for greater flexibility in terms of location. For this sumo bot, the ESP32 will be doing all the processing for camera, display and driving commands. The Arduino Uno R3 in addition to the ESP32 that will interface with the motor driver, sonar array and line detection. Between the two, there will be a connection established over I2c, however due to their differing logic voltages, a bidirectional logic level shifter will be implemented as the ESP32 operates at 3.3V whereas the Uno R3 uses 5V logic. The arduino is also needed as there are simply not enough connections on the ESP32 to directly connect all the systems. One could argue that an I2c based GPIO expander could be used however due to time constraints and the availability of UNO R3s as well as experience with the I2c interface; this was the solution applied – although yes, an expander of sorts could also be used. The “drivetrain” of the sumobot will be using standard, geared DC motors and motor driver. As for power, the entire bot will be powered using a 9v battery pack that will attach to a power distribution board to supply the appropriate voltages to the respective components.
